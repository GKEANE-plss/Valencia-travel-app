Valencia Through the Ages

From Roman colony to modern Mediterranean powerhouse — Valencia has one of the richest histories in Spain.

138 BC
Roman Valentia
Founded by the Roman consul Decimus Junius Brutus as Valentia Edetanorum, the city quickly became a prosperous Roman colony. You can still see the original Roman forum beneath the city — now displayed in the L'Almoina archaeological museum.
711–1238 AD
Moorish Balansiya
Following the Umayyad conquest of Iberia, Valencia — known as Balansiya — flourished under Moorish rule for over five centuries. The Moors introduced sophisticated irrigation systems (the famous Tribunal de las Aguas still meets today), transformed agriculture with rice and orange cultivation, and built a thriving cultural city. Arabic influences can still be felt in place names, food, and the street patterns of the old town.
1238
Christian Reconquest
King James I of Aragon (Jaume I) captured Valencia on 9 October 1238 — a date still celebrated as Valencia's national day. He transformed the city's mosque into the Cathedral and began rebuilding Valencia as a Christian kingdom. This date remains deeply important to Valencian identity.
14th–15th Century
The Golden Age of Commerce
Valencia entered its golden age as one of the most prosperous cities in the Mediterranean. The silk trade made it fabulously wealthy — hence the magnificent La Lonja de la Seda (Silk Exchange), completed in 1548. The city became a centre of Renaissance art, philosophy, and printing. The first book ever printed in Spain was produced in Valencia in 1474. Population exceeded 70,000 — enormous for medieval Europe.
1609
Expulsion of the Moriscos
King Philip III ordered the expulsion of the Moriscos (Muslim converts to Christianity), devastating the Valencian economy. Around 135,000 people were expelled from the Valencia region alone — roughly a third of the population. The agricultural economy, built on Moorish irrigation and expertise, collapsed almost overnight.
1700s
War of Spanish Succession
Valencia backed the losing side (the Habsburgs) in the War of Spanish Succession. Following the Bourbon victory, Philip V abolished the historic Valencian laws and privileges in 1707 via the Nueva Planta decrees, suppressing the Valencian language and regional autonomy — wounds that still resonate in Valencian culture and politics today.
19th–Early 20th Century
Industrial Revival & Las Fallas
Valencia industrialised rapidly through silk, orange exports, and ceramics. The famous Las Fallas festival — likely rooted in medieval carpenters' guilds burning wood shavings at the spring equinox — grew into the magnificent pyrotechnic spectacle it is today, declared Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity by UNESCO in 2016.
1936–1939
Civil War Capital
Valencia served briefly as the capital of the Spanish Republic during the Civil War (1936–39) after the government fled Madrid. The city was heavily bombed. Franco's victory ended the Republic, and Valencia endured decades of Francoist centralisation that suppressed Valencian language and culture.
1980s–2000s
Democracy & Reinvention
Following Spain's transition to democracy, Valencia was granted autonomy in 1982. The city famously rerouted the Turia River after devastating floods in 1957, converting the old riverbed into today's beloved Turia Gardens. The 1992 America's Cup, the 2007 Formula 1 European Grand Prix, and Santiago Calatrava's City of Arts & Sciences (completed 2009) transformed Valencia into a world-class destination.
Today
Spain's Third City
With a population of 800,000, Valencia is Spain's third-largest city — but increasingly seen as its most liveable. It was named World Design Capital in 2022 and regularly tops quality-of-life rankings. Its food scene, climate, beaches, and relatively affordable cost of living attract growing numbers of international residents and visitors every year.

Valencian Food & Drink

Valencia's culinary heritage is one of the richest in Spain — a product of Roman, Moorish, and Mediterranean influences across 2,000 years.

A Brief Food History

Valencia's food culture begins with the Romans, who cultivated wheat, grapes, and olive oil along the fertile coastal plain. The Moorish occupation transformed Valencian agriculture entirely — introducing rice cultivation to the wetlands of Albufera (creating the essential ingredient for paella), planting citrus groves that now define the landscape, and developing the sophisticated irrigation channels (acequias) that still water the region's fields today.


Paella itself was born in the rice paddies of Albufera in the 18th and 19th centuries — originally a working-class dish made by farmers cooking whatever was available: rice, rabbit, chicken, snails, and local green beans, over an open fire in a wide flat pan. The flat pan (paellera) allows maximum surface area for the prized socarrat — the caramelised crust of rice at the bottom. Valencia remains fiercely protective of the authentic recipe, and debates about which ingredients are "truly" Valencian are taken with great seriousness.

The Essential Dish

Paella Valenciana

The authentic original — chicken, rabbit, ferraura green beans, garrofó (large white beans), tomato, saffron, olive oil, and short-grain rice, cooked in a wide, shallow pan outdoors over an orange wood fire. Never seafood in the original. The socarrat (crispy caramelised bottom layer) is the mark of a perfectly cooked paella.

Unique to Valencia

Horchata (Orxata)

A sweet, milky-white drink made from tigernuts (chufas) — a tuber grown exclusively in the Huerta Valenciana. Creamy, slightly sweet, and refreshing, it's been made here since the Moorish period. Traditionally served ice-cold with long, sugar-dusted pastries called fartons. Not to be confused with Mexican horchata — this is the original.

Street & Market Food

Buñuelos de Calabaza

Pumpkin fritters — light, airy, and dusted with sugar. A Valencia specialty eaten especially during Las Fallas festival in March, but found in markets year-round. The combination of buñuelos dipped in hot chocolate is a beloved Valencian tradition.

Rice Variations

Arroz a Banda & Fideuà

Arroz a banda ("rice to one side") is a fisherman's dish — rice cooked in rich fish stock served separately from the seafood that made the broth. Fideuà is like paella but made with short vermicelli noodles instead of rice, with seafood and a rich fish stock. Both are deeply Valencian and worth trying alongside traditional paella.

The Citrus Capital

Valencia Oranges

Valencia's orange groves are famous worldwide — the Valencia orange variety is the world's most widely grown. Fresh-squeezed orange juice (zumo natural) is the standard breakfast drink. The combination of sweet citrus, rice, and seafood defines the Valencian kitchen.

Local Drinks

Agua de Valencia

Valencia's signature cocktail, invented at Café Madrid in 1959. A refreshing mix of fresh orange juice, cava (Catalan sparkling wine), vodka, and gin. Dangerously easy to drink and served ice-cold in large pitchers. The perfect evening aperitivo.

The Snack

All i Pebre

A fiery, garlicky stew made with eel from the Albufera lagoon, paprika, garlic, and almonds. One of the oldest Valencian dishes, pre-dating the arrival of tomatoes from the Americas. A robust, intensely flavoured dish eaten along the shores of Albufera — very much an acquired taste and a badge of local pride.

Artisan Spirits

Mistela & Licor de Café

Mistela is a traditional sweet Valencian wine made by arresting grape fermentation with the addition of alcohol — rich, fortified, and often drunk as a digestif. Licor de café (coffee liqueur) is another beloved Valencian after-dinner drink, served in small glasses and often paired with a piece of dark chocolate.

Local Wines

Valencia's wine scene is undergoing a quiet renaissance. With three distinct DOs — Valencia, Utiel-Requena, and Alicante — the region produces everything from powerful reds to crisp Mediterranean whites and legendary sweet Moscatel.

Wine Country

Valencia's vineyards stretch from the high-altitude plateaus of Utiel-Requena (600–900m) inland, where cool nights preserve freshness and acidity, down to the warm coastal plains where Moscatel thrives in Mediterranean sunshine. The region's winemakers are increasingly focused on indigenous varieties — Bobal, Merseguera, Verdil, and Monastrell — that express a genuine sense of place. Quality has risen sharply over the past 15 years while prices remain excellent value by Spanish standards.

🍷 Red Wines

Bobal

Valencia's flagship red grape, grown almost exclusively in the Utiel-Requena DO. Produces intensely violet-coloured wines with aromas of ripe blackberries, raspberries, and subtle earthy notes. On the palate: medium-to-full bodied with good natural acidity, smooth tannins, and a long finish. Best drunk slightly cool — a perfect partner to paella and grilled meats.

🍷 Red Wines

Monastrell (Mourvèdre)

Thrives in the warmest parts of the Valencia and Alicante DOs. Delivers intense aromas of dark fruits — blackcurrant, plum, dark chocolate — with herbal and spice notes. Full-bodied, powerful, and structured, with high alcohol and grippy tannins. The Alicante DO produces some of Spain's most exciting Monastrell, including the rare aged Fondillón dessert wine — the only one of its kind in the world.

🍷 Red Wines

Garnacha Tintorera & Tempranillo

Garnacha Tintorera (locally called Tintorera) is a rare red-fleshed grape that gives deeply coloured, rich wines with dark fruit and spice. Tempranillo — Spain's most planted variety — produces fresher, more elegant reds here than in Rioja: red cherry, leather, and subtle oak. Often blended with Cabernet Sauvignon or Merlot for structure and complexity.

🥂 White Wines

Merseguera

Valencia's most planted indigenous white variety. Produces light, fresh, and pleasantly aromatic wines with distinctive citrus and grassy notes — think lemon zest, white flowers, and a clean mineral finish. Light-bodied with good natural acidity. The ideal summer white and a natural match for the region's seafood and rice dishes. Best drunk young and well chilled.

🥂 White Wines

Moscatel de Valencia

The region's most celebrated white — a lusciously sweet, golden wine made from Moscatel de Alejandría grapes grown in the warm Moscatel subzone. Rich and unctuous with intense aromas of orange blossom, apricot, ripe peach, and honey. Traditionally served chilled as a dessert wine or aperitivo. Excellent value and deeply Valencian — look for it in any local bar or market.

🥂 White Wines

Verdil & Macabeo

Verdil is a rare indigenous white being revived by adventurous winemakers — fuller bodied than Merseguera, with stone fruit, floral notes, and a saline mineral quality that pairs beautifully with seafood. Macabeo (also the base for Cava) contributes freshness and crisp acidity to blends and single-varietal whites. Both are gaining attention from sommeliers across Spain.

Practical Info

Everything you need to know for a smooth trip — transport, money, apps, and emergency contacts.

🚕 Getting Around

🚕
Taxi apps — Download Free Now or Cabify before you arrive. Both work like Uber, give upfront pricing, and are widely available in Valencia. Standard street taxis are also plentiful — all are white with a red stripe and must accept card.
🚇
Metro & tram — Alameda station is 2 mins from your hotel. Line 1 covers most old town stops. Buy a reusable card (€1) and load it — up to 4 people can share one card. Runs 6am–midnight.
🚴
Bikes — The Turia Gardens are made for cycling. Hire from your hotel or Valenbisi (city bike scheme). Many rental shops near the hotel charge ~€8–12/day. Helmets recommended.
🎫
Valencia Tourist Card — Worth considering for a 3-day stay. Covers unlimited public transport + free entry to Torres de Serranos, IVAM, La Almoina, and discounts at many attractions including the Oceanogràfic. Buy at the airport kiosk on arrival. 72-hour card ~€25.

💶 Money & Tipping

🏧
ATMs — Widely available throughout the city. Avoid airport ATMs (poor rates). Use ATMs attached to banks (BBVA, Sabadell, CaixaBank) for best rates. Most restaurants and shops accept card — contactless is universal.
💡
Tipping — Not obligatory in Spain, but appreciated. Rounding up the bill or leaving €1–2 per person at sit-down restaurants is perfectly normal. No expectation to tip at bars or market stalls.
🧾
Meal times — Spaniards eat late. Lunch is 2–4pm (the main meal of the day), dinner rarely starts before 9pm. Restaurants often won't seat you for dinner before 8:30pm. Don't fight it — lean in.

📱 Useful Apps

🗺
Google Maps — Download Valencia offline before you travel. Works without internet for walking and transit directions.
🚕
Free Now / Cabify — Taxi apps. More reliable than hailing off the street, and you see the price upfront.
🍽
TheFork (ElTenedor) — Book restaurants online. All four of your restaurants are on it — book as soon as possible, especially for weekends.

💡 Good to Know

🗣
Language — Signs are in both Spanish and Valencian (similar to Catalan). Hospitality staff almost all speak English. A few words of Spanish go a long way: gracias (thank you), por favor (please), la cuenta (the bill).
🔌
Plugs — Spain uses Type C/F plugs (round two-pin, 230V). Bring an EU adapter if coming from Ireland/UK.
☀️
Sun — Even in April, Valencia's sun is strong. Wear SPF on exposed skin, especially during long walks around the old town.
👜
Pickpockets — Low risk in Valencia compared to Barcelona or Madrid, but stay aware around the Cathedral, Central Market, and on the metro. Keep bags in front of you in crowded areas.

Shopping in Valencia

From luxury boutiques on the Golden Mile to vintage finds in Ruzafa and artisan crafts in El Carmen — Valencia is a serious shopping city.

🕐 General hours: Mon–Sat 10am–9pm. Most independent shops close for siesta 2–5pm. Shops generally closed Sundays — plan Saturday for shopping. Large chains (Zara, H&M, El Corte Inglés) stay open all day.

🎁 Valencian Souvenirs Worth Buying

🏺
Hand-painted ceramics & azulejo tiles — Valencia has a 700-year ceramic tradition. Look for hand-painted tiles and bowls in El Carmen and Plaça Redonda. Far better quality than tourist-shop versions.
🍊
Valencian food products — Saffron, rice (Bomba, Senia), horchata powder, Moscatel wine, local olive oil. All available at Mercado Central or Colmado LaLola in Ruzafa.
👜
Spanish leather goods — Loewe is the ultimate, but there are many quality leather shops in the old town at more accessible prices. Valencia has a long leather-working tradition.
🪭
Fans (abanicos) — Traditional hand-painted Valencian fans are made locally and make beautiful gifts. Look for hand-made versions in Plaça Redona — avoid mass-produced tourist versions.
🧺
Wicker baskets — Carrer del Músic Peydró ("Street of the Baskets") has sold handwoven Valencian wicker baskets for centuries. Practical, beautiful, and genuinely local.
💡 Sunday shopping: Most independent shops are CLOSED on Sundays. El Corte Inglés and some chains in the centre may open with reduced hours. Best shopping days are Friday afternoon and all day Saturday.

Bars & Nightlife

From neo-baroque cocktail palaces to hidden speakeasies and a legendary jazz club — Valencia's bar scene is one of the best in Spain.

🕐 Valencia drinks late. Bars come alive from 9pm, peak around 11pm–1am. Clubs don't fill until after midnight. Most bars open until 2–3am on weekends.

🍸 Cocktail Bars

Café de las Horas
Must Visit
📍 Carrer del Comte d'Almodóvar, 1 · El Carmen 🕐 Daily 10am–2am

One of Valencia's most iconic bars — a neo-baroque, utterly theatrical space dripping with red velvet, chandeliers, sculptures and flowers. Frequently called the most beautiful bar in Spain. Famous as the best place in the city for Agua de Valencia, served in half-litre or full-litre carafes. Has been featured in films and TV shows. Go early evening before the queues build.

🍊 Order: Agua de Valencia (served by the carafe) · Signature cocktails · Excellent coffee
Apotheke Speakeasy
Speakeasy
📍 C. Císcar, 26, Bajo Izquierda · Eixample 🕐 Mon/Wed–Thu 7pm–1:30am · Fri–Sat 5pm–1:30am · Sun 7pm–1:30am · Closed Tue

Valencia's first and finest speakeasy — inspired by Prohibition-era America and consistently ranked among Spain's top 50 bars. Hidden behind an unmarked door; you need the daily password to enter (get it by booking online). 1920s-inspired interior with low lighting, live jazz music, and an extraordinary cocktail menu. Whisky Sour with bacon and Parmesan bitters, smoky Mezcal Paloma — nothing is ordinary here. Oysters and small plates also available. Book well in advance.

🔑 Tip: Password required — book via apothekevlc.com. Reservations essential at weekends.
The Green Book
World's 50 Best
📍 Ruzafa neighbourhood 🕐 Evenings Wed–Sun

A secret bar disguised as a bookshop in Ruzafa — the sign outside reads Librería and nothing suggests a bar within. Step inside and find yourself in a Hogwarts-meets-speakeasy world: floor-to-ceiling bookshelves, sofas, tables and a bar hidden among the stacks. Featured in the World's 50 Best Bars list in 2026. The cocktail menu resembles a velvet-bound diary. Signature drinks are all creative originals. One of the most talked-about bars in Spain right now.

📖 Tip: Find it in Ruzafa — look for the bookshop front. No sign of a bar outside. Best visited with a reservation.
Café Madrid
Historic
📍 Carrer de Abadía de Sant Martí, 2 · El Carmen 🕐 Mon–Thu 9:30am–1am · Fri 9:30am–2am · Sat 10am–2am · Sun 10am–1am

This is the birthplace of Agua de Valencia — the legendary cocktail was invented here in 1959 and this bar has been the hub of Valencia's bohemian and artistic life ever since. A heritage site of the city. Beyond the classic Agua de Valencia, the bar now offers 11 new signature cocktails inspired by Valencian landscapes — from marshes to volcanoes. Old Fashioned made with Woodford Reserve (€18), Hugo Spritz with cava (€12), and the locally inspired Un paseo por la Albufera using Valencian gin M'Alegra. A must-visit for history alone.

🍊 Order: The original Agua de Valencia · Valencian gin cocktail · Any of the 11 signature creations
Borgia Wine Bar
Terrace
📍 Calle dels Cavallers · El Carmen 🕐 Daily from 6pm

On the iconic Calle dels Cavallers — the main artery of El Carmen — this elegant wine bar has a terrace perfect for warm evenings and a cocktail menu celebrating Renaissance hedonism (fitting for a Borgia-themed bar in a city with deep Spanish history). Excellent wine list alongside creative cocktails. Great spot for a long, relaxed evening drink in the heart of the old town.
